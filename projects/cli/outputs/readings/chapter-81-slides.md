Study Title: The Safety of Obedience

---

Slide 1 Question: How does God compare religious rituals with actual obedience to His voice?

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of the prophet Samuel standing sternly before King Saul, soft natural light illuminating their faces, tents in the background.

---

Slide 2 Biblical Answer: “Hath the Lord as great delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices, as in obeying the voice of the Lord? Behold, to obey is better than sacrifice, and to hearken than the fat of rams.” (1 Samuel 15:22)

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of a sacrificial altar with smoke rising, but the focus is on a figure turning away from the ritual to listen to the sky.

---

Slide 3 Explanation: King Saul attempted to cover his partial disobedience by offering sacrifices. Samuel clarifies that religious activity—sacrifices, services, or offerings—is never a substitute for doing what God asked. God desires a surrendered heart more than religious ceremony.

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of a king looking down in shame, his royal robes contrasting with his humbled posture, soft light.

---

Slide 4 Did You Know? The "fat of rams" was considered the choicest, most valuable part of the sacrificial animal. Samuel was saying that even giving God the very best material gifts is worthless if we are simultaneously refusing to listen to Him.

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of a ram caught in a thicket, detailed texture, soft golden lighting highlighting the animal.

---

Slide 5 Question: What excuse is often given for compromising God's commands?

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of a crowd of ancient soldiers talking amongst themselves, with one man looking anxiously at them.

---

Slide 6 Biblical Answer: “I have transgressed the commandment of the Lord, and thy words; because I feared the people, and obeyed their voice.” (1 Samuel 15:24)

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of King Saul gesturing toward a crowd of people, appearing pressured and fearful, soft dramatic lighting.

---

Slide 7 Explanation: Saul admitted that peer pressure—the fear of public opinion—caused him to compromise. He valued the voice of the crowd more than the voice of God.

[ILL] Imagine a builder who follows the blueprints perfectly until the neighbors shout that they prefer the wall in a different spot. If the builder listens to the neighbors instead of the architect, the house may collapse. We are building a life, and God is the Architect.

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of an ancient builder holding a scroll blueprint, looking conflicted as people point in different directions.

---

Slide 8 Question: How strictly does God require us to stick to His written Word?

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of Moses holding a scroll with great reverence, soft light glowing on the parchment.

---

Slide 9 Biblical Answer: “Ye shall not add unto the word which I command you, neither shall ye diminish aught from it, that ye may keep the commandments of the Lord your God which I command you.” (Deuteronomy 4:2)

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of a scribe carefully writing on a scroll, a quill in hand, illuminated by a single oil lamp.

---

Slide 10 Explanation: We are not to "edit" God's instructions. We should not add extra burdens God never required, nor delete commandments that feel inconvenient.

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of an ancient scroll lying open on a wooden table, with nothing adding to or taking away from its presence.

---

Slide 11 Term Definition: "Strange Fire" (Leviticus 10:1) refers to unauthorized fire used by Nadab and Abihu in the sanctuary. God specified the coals were to come from the altar, but they substituted common fire. This illustrates that when God specifies a method, He expects us to follow it, not substitute our preferences.

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of two priests holding censers with strange, erratic fire, contrasting with the holy light of the sanctuary.

---

Slide 12 Question: If a religious leader or "prophet" tells us something that contradicts what God has already commanded, whom should we obey?

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of an old prophet speaking to a younger man under a large oak tree, soft dappled sunlight.

---

Slide 13 Biblical Answer: “Thus saith the Lord, Forasmuch as thou has disobeyed the mouth of the Lord... and hast eaten bread and drunk water... thy carcass shall not come unto the sepulcher of thy fathers.” (1 Kings 13:21-22)

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of a man sitting at a table eating bread, looking suddenly realized and sorrowful, soft lighting.

---

Slide 14 Explanation: In 1 Kings 13, a "man of God" had specific orders not to eat in a certain place. An older prophet lied, claiming an angel said it was allowed. The man of God was punished because he trusted the word of a man—even a claiming prophet—over the clear Word of God.

[SN] This narrative reinforces the principle that the Bible stands above church tradition, feelings, or the claims of religious dignitaries. If a "new message" contradicts the established "written Word," the written Word must always prevail.

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of a lion and a donkey standing quietly next to a fallen man on a dusty road, historically respectful and somber.

---

Slide 15 Question: Jesus describes two foundations for life. Who builds on the rock?

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of a rugged coastline with a house built firmly on a high stone cliff.

---

Slide 16 Biblical Answer: “Therefore whosoever heareth these sayings of mine, and doeth them, I will liken him unto a wise man, which built his house upon a rock... And every one that heareth these sayings of mine, and doeth them not, shall be likened unto a foolish man, which built his house upon the sand.” (Matthew 7:24, 26)

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting depicting a storm raging against a house on a rock (secure) and a house on sand (collapsing), dramatic lighting.

---

Slide 17 Explanation: The difference between the wise and foolish man was not their knowledge—both "heard" the sayings. The difference was action. Obedience acts as an anchor. When storms come, only the life constructed on doing God's will remains standing.

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of a man carrying stones to build a foundation, sweat on his brow, determined expression.

---

Slide 18 Deeper Insight: Many today unknowingly build on sand regarding the Fourth Commandment. The text reads, "The seventh day is the Sabbath of the Lord," yet tradition substitutes the first day. Like the "Man of God" in 1 Kings 13, we are safer following exactly what God wrote rather than popular tradition.

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of the stone tablets of the Ten Commandments, with soft light highlighting the fourth commandment.

---

Slide 19 Practical Application:
• **Audit our inputs:** Are we listening to culture more than Scripture?
• **Check our foundations:** Are we ignoring clear commands (like the Sabbath or honesty) because they are inconvenient?
• **Take action:** Faith is moving our feet in the direction God points.

[ILL] A map is useless if you refuse to turn the steering wheel. The Bible is the map; obedience is the driving. God wants to take us safely home.

[IMG] Warm classical biblical painting of a traveler looking at a scroll map on a journey, pointing toward a distant city on a hill.
